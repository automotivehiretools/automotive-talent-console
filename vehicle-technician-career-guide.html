How to Become a Vehicle Technician (Car Mechanic)

A simple, no-nonsense guide for anyone who wants to work as a vehicle technician in a garage, dealership or bodyshop – including skills, qualifications and where to find jobs.

Guide type: Career · Audience: Candidates and career switchers · Works globally

1. What does a Vehicle Technician do?

Vehicle technicians (sometimes called car mechanics or auto technicians) inspect, service and repair cars and light vehicles. Depending on the employer, the work can range from basic servicing to complex diagnostics and electrical faults.

Typical tasks include:

  • Carrying out routine servicing, MOT preparation and safety checks.
  • Diagnosing faults using scan tools and test drives.
  • Repairing braking systems, suspension, steering and exhausts.
  • Replacing clutches, timing belts, engines and gearboxes.
  • Updating job cards and speaking with service advisors or customers.
Good for: hands-on people Workplaces: garages, dealers, bodyshops

2. Skills & qualifications

Core skills

  • Good understanding of how petrol and diesel engines work.
  • Basic electrical knowledge and fault finding.
  • Safe use of tools and workshop equipment.
  • Ability to follow repair instructions and wiring diagrams.
  • Attention to detail and strong focus on safety.

Qualifications (examples)

You don’t need every qualification below – these are common examples:

  • Apprenticeship as a light vehicle technician.
  • NVQ / SVQ / equivalent Level 2–3 automotive qualification.
  • Brand training from a dealership (e.g. Ford, VW, BMW, etc.).
  • Inspection or MOT tester licence, depending on country.

3. Typical salary ranges (high-level)

Salary varies a lot by country, experience, brand and bonus structure. As a rough guide, many markets follow a pattern like:

  • Apprentice / improver – usually entry-level pay for the region.
  • Qualified technician – mid-range pay plus some bonus or overtime.
  • Senior / master technician – higher base plus stronger bonus scheme.

To get a realistic figure for your location, use recent job adverts and salary surveys as your main reference.

4. How to get started with no experience

  • Look for apprentice, trainee or improver roles at local garages and dealers.
  • Consider a college course that includes real workshop time.
  • Offer to help in smaller garages to gain basic experience, if allowed.
  • Build a simple CV focused on attitude, reliability and any hands-on hobbies.
  • Apply widely – bodyshops, fast-fit centres and smaller workshops often train.
